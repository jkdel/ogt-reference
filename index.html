Background

The Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS) consists in a range of metabolic disorders predisposing horses and ponies for laminitis. The term Insulin dysregulation describes an exacerbated insulin response to orally available sugars and may therefore involve incretin signalling. It is one of the central components of EMS alongside with regional or generalized obesity.

Insulin resistance results from a damaged response of peripheral tissues to insulin and is asociated with insulin dysregulation. It is however not present in every horse suffering from insulin dysregulation, nor is every horse having an insulin resistance insulin dysregulated. This is why diagnostical tests for EMS should not rely solely on insulin-sensitivity.

The Oral Glucose Test (OGT) consists in the administration of 1 g glucose/kg bodyweight in 2L of water via nasogastric tubing. Blood samples for insulin measurments are drawn prior to the tubing and 120 min after it. In contrast to tests like the Combined Glucose/Insulin Test (CGIT), this test has the advantages of being more practical to perform in a field environnement and to activate incretin signaling. The activation of incretin signaling is not achieved by intravenous glucose infusion, this why the OGT is probably better suited to detect insulin dysregulation, while the other tests are more relevant for insulin sensitivity.

This Webapp is based on a paper which tries to provide a more consistent reference range for insulin during the OGT. Further, by modelling the insulin answer of approximately 30 horses considered healthy during the OGT, it is capable of alleviating the requirements on timing. Actually, drawing the second blood sample exactly 120 min after the tubing might not be achievable outside of a hospital. This App provides a reference for insulin at any time-point between 90 and 180 minutes. These references are currently only available for insulin measured using the Mercodia Equine Insulin ELISA.

More informations

Insulin dysregulation is a central part of the Equine Metabolic Syndrome and can be detected using an Oral Glucose Test. However it might also occur in horses affected by Pars Pituitary Intermedia Deficiency (PPID), formerly known as Equine Cushing Syndrome. This is why horses that where found positiv in the OGT and could possibly be affected by PPID (age, other clinical signs) should also be tested, and if necessary treated, for this disease.

The managment of patients with EMS mostly consists in an optimisation of the diet and exercise. Energy intake has to be reduced gradually and adapted to the horse's actual needs, which are sometimes difficult to evaluate for the owner. Exercising of the horse is not limited to a more intense training but also includes changes in the stables like access to a paddock, more space for social interaction or putting obstacles in the paddock to make the route from roughage to water more circuitous etc. More specific advices and suggestions can be found in literatur.

For more details about the protocol recommended for use with this reference values and the dataset used to suggest these references please read following paper that was the starting point for writing this webapp:
Warnken T, Delarocque J, Schumacher S, Huber K, Feige K. Determination of reference ranges for oral glucose test (OGT) performed with naso-gastric tubing in horses. Acta Vet Scand. 2017.
